### **The 10 Riskiest Assumptions You're Making, Ranked by Impact**

Based on our entire discovery sprint, here are the 10 assumptions that, if proven wrong, could have the biggest negative impact on your project.

1.  **The "Alpha" Assumption:** The single biggest assumption is that your proprietary ML models can **consistently generate real, predictive "alpha"** that gives users a genuine edge. If the signals are not effective, the entire product has no value.
    
2.  **The "Willingness to Pay" Assumption:** You are assuming that "Alex," a user accustomed to a world of free crypto tools, **will pay a recurring $29/month subscription.** He might prefer to patch together free tools or decide your insights aren't valuable enough to pay for, killing the business model.
    
3.  **The "Backtester is the Key to Trust" Assumption:** The entire strategy for building trust hinges on the "No-Code Backtester." What if users don't care about backtesting? What if they find it too complicated and all they really want are simple, trustworthy signals, making your core feature irrelevant?
    
4.  **The "Market Size" Assumption:** You are assuming that "Alex, the Proactive Retail Trader" represents a **large enough market segment** to build a venture-scale business. This persona might be a niche within a niche, making it difficult to scale user acquisition.
    
5.  **The "Go-to-Market" Assumption:** You are assuming that you can **effectively and affordably reach "Alex"** through content marketing and community engagement (Reddit, Discord). These channels are incredibly noisy, and the Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) might end up being far too high to be profitable.
    
6.  **The "MVP is Feasible in 6 Weeks" Assumption:** You are assuming that a small team can build a performant, intuitive backtesting MVP in just 6 weeks. Data pipelines are complex, and user expectations for speed are high. Technical delays could burn through your initial runway before you even get to market.
    
7.  **The "Explainable AI is the Moat" Assumption:** You assume that transparency (XAI) is your competitive advantage. A competitor with a "black box" model that simply produces better results could still win. Users might prioritize raw performance over explainability.
    
8.  **The "Platform Dependency" Assumption:** The product relies heavily on third-party data sources (like the Twitter/X API, on-chain data providers). A key provider could suddenly change their terms, raise prices prohibitively, or shut off access, crippling your product overnight.
    
9.  **The "Freemium Conversion" Assumption:** You're assuming a freemium model will effectively convert free users to paid subscribers. It's possible the free version is "good enough" for most users, or that it's too limited to properly showcase the value of the Pro features, leading to low conversion rates.
    
10.  **The "Problem Framing" Assumption:** You're assuming the core problem is a "lack of confidence." While likely true, what if the bigger, more urgent problem for users is simply a "lack of time" or a "lack of capital"? This might change the optimal solution from an analytics tool to something else entirely.
